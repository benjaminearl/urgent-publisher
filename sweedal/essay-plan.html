Indigenous Weather Forecasting: Systems of knowledge that decolonize the Anthropos

Thesis Statement

This essay explores the phenomenon of indigenous weather forecasting through the wider lens of environmental philosophies, climate awareness and earth system sciences. We will also consider the anti-discourse to the idea of centering of the human through decolonizing the Western-oriented influence of the Anthropos.

Chapter 1: Anthropocentrism – A perceived human drive to dominate or master the Earth

Anthropocentrism is the belief that human beings are the central or most important entity on the planet.[2] Some refer to the concept as human supremacy or human exceptionalism. From an anthropocentric perspective, humankind is seen as separate from nature and superior to it, and other entities (animals, plants, minerals, etc.) are viewed as resources for humans to use.

Chapter 2: Gaia Theory and General System Theory

Gaia Theory suggests that the Earth is a self-regulating complex system involving multiple kinds of ecological spheres tightly coupled as an evolving system, and the General System Theory, when applied to ecology, focuses on interactions and transactions within and between biological and ecological systems, and is especially concerned with the way the functioning of ecosystems can be influenced by human interventions.

Chapter 3: Relational ontology - the relationship comes first

Indigenous peoples understand reality through putting relationships between humans, land, animals, and ancestors at the centre. The most fundamental clash between Western and indigenous belief systems, in Mander's view, stems from a belief held by indigenous peoples that the earth is a living entity, Mother Earth. From this belief, indigenous values and practices relations are derived, which place indigenous views in direct opposition to Western values.

Conclusion

The phenomenon of indigenous weather forecasting proves to be not just a discourse on the idea of decentralising the human, but provides a lens that Western thought is not the universal legitimate knowledge, and learning Indigenous Climate Knowledge ethically could help us to be more aware of the challenges raised by Climate change.
