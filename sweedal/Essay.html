Indigenous Weather Forecasting along with Sensory Attunement: Systems of knowledge that decolonize the Anthropos

Introduction

Cultures from all over the world have developed different views of nature throughout human history. According to the 2007 United Nations (UN), there is no definition of the term ‘Indigenous peoples,’ which is owing to their diversity. A lot of traditional systems of beliefs are unique to indigenous people and local communities, because they are rooted in lived experiences and connected to their biophysical environment which they use to understand engage with their environment. Traditional practices wisdom developed over many generations of holistic traditional utilization of the lands, natural resources and environment. This knowledge is generally passed down from generation to generations by experiential learning and word of mouth, and is importantly most part, undocumented in written form (Thounaojam et al. 2019). With the onset of climate change and weather changes this knowledge proves to be vital for understanding and protecting life on the planet.

Therefore, in this essay, we will explore the phenomenon of indigenous weather forecasting through the wider lens of environmental philosophies, climate awareness and earth system sciences. We will also consider the anti-discourse through decolonizing the Western-oriented influence of the Anthropos.



Chapter 1: A perceived human drive to dominate or master the Earth

Indigenous peoples and local communities (IPLCs) hold long histories of place-based living, developed and accumulated time-honored traditions that produced sophisticated knowledge and practices about the natural world around them (Dovchin et al. 2023). IPLCs’ knowledge systems, transmitted through generations as ancestral wisdom, emphasis sustaining profound, reciprocal, and responsible relationships among humans, their non-human kin and the natural world. They are rooted in lived experiences and connected to IPLCs’ cosmology, ontology, governance, culture, language, and religion (Dovchin et al. 2023). This results in the indigenous knowledge systems being extremely valuable for understanding and protecting life on the planet. From a political perspective, the notion of ‘anthropos’ - ‘humanity at large’ - embedded within the discourse of the Anthropocene is still rooted in human agency and taking humanity as the dominant reference point in understanding the world (Jeong et al.,2021: 808). According to Posthumanism, humans are not the sole/only species capable of producing knowledge, and when human are decentred as the sole knowledge producers, a wealth of alternative knowledge emerges (Ulmer, 2017). Posthumanism also emphasises that human and nature while being interconnected, they transcend the notion that human exceptionalism defines nature. Indigenous cosmology is, therefore, more than a theory of creation about the origin and structure of the world or universe; it encompasses a theory of human participation and action (Johnson, 2014). For instance, a crucial observation is the ethical and respectful relationship that traditional communities maintain with animals and their environment. Many elders spoke not only of observing animals but also of treating them as “messengers” or “helpers, indicating a deeper cultural philosophy of coexistence (Rohiwal et al. 2025). Chakma Indigenous belief systems portray mountains as sacred places that serve as mediators between the human and spirit worlds, playing significant roles in human and non-human lives (Chakma and Sultana, 2023). Which further proves that being intune with your environment and being an equal part of it instead having the belief of being a dominant species in it is not the fundamental teaching of IK and to learn IPLC’s knowledge we need to step out from the western framework of the Anthropocentrism. The connection between anthropocentrism and environmental degradation is because ‘what people do about their ecology depends upon what they think about themselves in relation to things around them’ (White 1967). And Indigenous Americans on the whole regarded their relationship with nature as essentially moral or ethical: ‘Animals, plants and minerals were treated as persons and conceived to be coequal members of a natural social order’ (Callicott and Overholt 2003: 63). As Jeong et al. (2021) emphasise, ‘life in all its forms, from micro to macro, is about relationships with cultural and natural ecologies. Any changes in these relationships can lead to the sustaining, altering, or threatening of these ecologies.’ Australian Indigenous Aboriginal cosmology emphasises that humanity’s challenge is to sustain the ecological balance, being ultimately responsible for the continuous harmony between nature and culture, harmony and balance being at the heart of the health and continuity of both systems. The sustaining effects are reciprocal in that if humans take care of nature, acting as custodians of the continuity of diverse species, natural abundance is assured for the continuity of humans and their cultures. For instance, following ancestral Mongolian cosmology, the fate of humankind is inextricably interwoven with the fate of the natural world itself. Accordingly, the land does not belong to humans; humans belong to the land, which is embodied in the Mongolian proverb: ‘humans, land and the natural world are connected through an umbilical cord’. Land, non-human beings, and the natural world, therefore, are living and sentient entities with identity, agency and intentionality, neither a resource nor a commodity to be owned and exploited, but revered, nurtured, and sustained in a holistic, spiritually and physically entwined, reciprocal, and symbiotic relationship (Dovchin and Dovchin, 2023). For instance, Aboriginal desert dwellers in Australia consider that without performing appropriate ceremonies rain would not occur as they consider the rain to be as dependent on them as they are on it (Rose. 1956).



Chapter 2: The relationship comes first

The most fundamental clash between Western and indigenous belief systems, in Jenny Mander's view, stems from a belief held by indigenous peoples that the earth is a living entity, Mother Earth. The problem lies in the exclusionary scope of voices and beings heeded and represented by current dominant climate imaginaries–imaginaries that remain firmly anchored in, and perpetuate, the logic of human mastery over a “nature” recast as a passive, material substrate, meaningful only to the extent that it is useful to (certain) humans (Plumwood, 1993; Singh, 2018). The environment itself is composed of diverse inter-species and interelemental relations. Each actant within it plays a consequential role in shaping the worlds of others–from subterranean microbes and fungi to oxygen-producing trees, ground-churning mammalian bioturbators, and pollinating birds and insects. There are no individual or autonomous actors within this world; rather, there are multiple, intra-active companionships bound in a relational ethic of kinship and care (Arabena, 2010; Hau’ofa, 2008). It also challenges us to expand what we mean by “beyond human” to include not just other “life” forms, or bios, but also elemental bodies such as oceans, rivers, mountains, and soils, that many Indigenous peoples consider to be equally animate and sentient (Argyrou & Hummels, 2019; Bawaka Country etal., 2015; Martuwarra River of Life et al., 2020). It also demands to reposition the ‘human’ within a broader sense. Stories offer a potent medium for (re)imagining the climate relationally and beyond the human. In West Papua, where Chaohas been conducting research among Indigenous Marind people, species do not exist as categories but rather as stories. Rather, species come into being through richly complex and orally transmitted narratives that flesh out their origins, movements, subsistence, and social and emotive lives. Individual plants and animals have stories, as do plant and animal collectives, ecosystems, and even entire territories. Together, humans and other-than-humans partake in one ongoing and constantly transforming multispecies story. Taking a multi-sensory narrative to the environment around us also involves engaging through our senses rather than adopting a distanced perspective of the world and base the narrative on which all life forms arises and transform. In anthropologist Anna Tsing’s (2014) terms, that we learn to “look around” and not just “look ahead.” This art of sensory attunement involves a certain slowing down of thought and movement. As the famous Marind proverb goes, “Stop talking, start listening. Stop thinking, start walking.” What Marind are calling for here is a silencing of the self that makes space for the voices of others –the ripple of a river, the shy whistle of a bird, or the gentle sway of a palm frond. This multispecies sensorium is powerfully captured also in the words of Kabi, an Indigenous Gimi man living just across the border from Marind in Papua New Guinea. When asked by environmental anthropologist Paige West how he knows the forest, Kabi responds: “My eyes, my ears, my nose,my mouth, my teeth, my skin, my father, my bones” (West, 2006, p. 237). For instance, In traditional weather forecasting, the onset of the rainy season and upcoming rain is also indicated by the unusual behavior of certain birds and animals aggressive and abnormal activities. So like Kabi, we should learn to know the climate through the nature around us - our bodily flesh and fluids, the air that we breathe, the waters that nourish us, and the soils that hold us. Every sensory receptor matters to every form of matter because it allows us to gain a better understanding of each other. For instance, Elder, scholar, and activist Manulani Aluli-Meyer (2001) describes listening as central to paying attention to the rhythms and patterns of the more-than-human world and to becoming actively aware and respectful of its spiritual force and presence. In Australian IK it is said that when average number of cows are lying down in a field, rain is on its way as the cows sense the moisture in the air and are making sure they have somewhere dry to lie down. Which means they sense the moisture of approaching rain and make sure they have a dry patch to lie on further proving the narrative and teaching in IK of sensory attunement. Around the world, IK has a similar pattern to weather forecasting which is based on animal behaviour (birds, insects , wild animals), plant and trees, human body, wind and star-moon alignment. IPLC’s belief that the earth is a living entity that self regulates itself with/without the need of human intervention. And in Western thought, Gaia Theory suggests that the Earth is a self-regulating complex system involving multiple kinds of ecological spheres tightly coupled as an evolving system, while the General System Theory, when applied to ecology, focuses on interactions and transactions within and between biological and ecological systems, and is especially concerned with the way the functioning of ecosystems can be influenced by human interventions. So, while western though has discovered the concept of earth being a self-regulating system, it does not necessarily agree that it’s functioning should not be influenced by humans. According to research, Folk weather lore and practices have remained to be important form of local weather forecasting, which say sensory attunement is quite essential in traditional ways of weather forecasting. For instance, The possibility and beginning of the rainyseason in Bishnupur is signaled by the unusually chirping of the Apis dorsata Fabricius, Bos taurus Linnaeus, Bufomelanostictus Schneider, Centropus toulou bengalensis Gmelin, Cuculus canorus Linnaeus, Megalaima asiatica,Megalaima australis Cyanotis Melanochlora sultanea Hodgson, Odontotermes sp., Papilio Linnaeus, , Picus canusGmelin, Pycnonotus jocosus Linnaeus and Treron cuvirostra nipalensis (Thounaojam et al. 2019). The reason is also that IK is fundamentally structured on relative experience and local experience and only be learnt through something called as experiential living. Like Occurrence of large flocks of swallows and swans roaming from the South to North during the months of September–November, for example, is an indication of onset of short rain sometimes within two to three days, while sighting of an owl in the sky means it is planting season. Migration of bees during season of resource abundance from north to south is also a sign of drought, while migration in the reverse direction indicates a normal rainfall season. Appearance of insects locally known as vidododo on an Albizia tree with water dripping from the trees during a dry season is an indication of onset of short-rain seasons. In Lushoto, Tanzania, the occurrence of a halo moon (moon surrounded by a yellow ring), moon with different colors like rainbow, and appearance of moon in a circular shape (normal) are all indicators of onset of rains for long-rain and short-rain seasons. Moon surrounded with heavy clouds is a sign of a good rainfall season. Also, the moon orientation can be used to predict a drought season. A slanted position of the moon crescent is an indicator of a drought season, with less rain expected on the inclined side. Changes in the human like Palpitation on top of the right eye and one-sided headaches, for example, are also considered an indication of onset of rains and a high birth rate of male children and animals in a particular season is usually taken as a sign of a likely drought season. And all of these observations are not based on measurement of time, IPLC watched for changes in their environment. Because most Aboriginal languages, at least in Australia, did not have a numbering system with terms beyond five, although if Aboriginal people needed to, they could count higher in multiples of five (Harris Citation1987, Tindale Citation1978). Daytime was marked by the position of the sun, while the number of days and moons accounted for longer periods (Clarke 2009). Despite the presence of modern technology to predict weather conditions over the next day or month in a specific location (Thounaojam et al. 2019), IK gives us knowledge to master the skill of weather pattern predictability and pathways to add to an active, evolving knowledge to predict weather patterns and climate change better.



Conclusion

The phenomenon of indigenous weather forecasting proves to be not just a discourse on the idea of decentralising the human, but provides a lens that Western thought is not the universal legitimate knowledge, and learning Indigenous Climate Knowledge ethically could help us to be more aware of the challenges raised by Climate change. And integrating this knowledge could also help us tackle climate change and forecast weather better while also encouraging communities to be more in touch with their environment and develop a sensory attunement towards it. A point of consideration while learning Indigenous Climate Knowledge is also a careful consideration towards ethics, and while I’m aware that a point of critique could be that the knowledge could act as a way of extraction and a beneficiary of colonization.
