Multisensory Preceptions and Indigenious Knowledges

When Kabi, an Indigenous Gimi man, was asked by environmental anthropologist Paige West how he knows the forest, he responded with, “My eyes, my ears, my nose, my mouth, my teeth, my skin, my father, my bones”. In Indigenous Knowledge, the art of sensory attunement is not just essential but also the only way to understand and learn it. Every sensory receptor matters to every form of matter because it allows us to gain a better understanding of each other. And multi-sensory imagination calls for a phenomenological immersion in the material worlds that we are part of –its textures, smells, sounds, sights, and movements. And I wonder if language and dialect could be explored more with multi-sensory imagination as a lens. According to the researchers, it is easier to learn vocabulary if the brain can link a given word with different sensory perceptions. This suggests that the brain learns foreign words more easily when they are associated with information from different sensory organs. It may be that these associations are mutually reinforcing, imprinting the source-language term and its translation more deeply in the mind. In other words, to learn the Spanish word for apple, the subject should make an apple gesture, taste an apple or look at a picture of an apple. So maybe we could archive and document indigenous and local languages and the knowledge that the language holds through different sensory perceptions to not just make this knowledge easier to learn for younger generations that might need it, but also accessible for the world to create an institutional system where all kinds of knowledge are universal and Western knowledge is not the only one that is the centre of legitimate knowledge.
